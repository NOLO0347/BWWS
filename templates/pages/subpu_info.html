Submersible Pumps

What is a Submersible Pump?

Like it's name suggests, a submersible well pump is installed inside your home's water well, submerged under the water line. Designed for wells that run 50 feet deep or more, their depth allows them to push water up into a pipe that delivers the water to your storage tank.


Advantages of a submersible pump

The number one benefit to this kind of well pump is its efficiency. Because the pump is already in the water, it's able to push the water up which uses energy more effectively. This offers several benefits including:

  • Durability - They don't have to work as hard to move water into the home.
  • Speed - There's already water in the pump, so there's no need to prime it to get water into the house, saving time.
  • Pressure - A submersible pump provides stronger, more consistent water pressure.

    • Additionally, submersible pumps are generally convenient because they are portable and can be moved as needed, don't take up any property space outside the well, and run more quietly than jet pumps.

Disadvantages of a Submersible Pump

Submersibles need to go directly into the well, so they are a bit more complicated to install, and should only be put in by experienced well pump technicians. Also, while it's unlikely that they will overheat because of the continuous flow of water around the pump, heat can accumulate within the compartment which can lead to a risk of overheating. The biggest drawback to a submersible pump is the cost of purchase and well pump maintenance is greater than a standard pump.

The Difference Between 2-Wire and 3-Wire Submersible Pumps

For any type of electric deep well pump, the main differences in the wiring will depend on the horsepower of the motor running on the pump, and where the starting components on the pump are located (like the relay and start capacitor). Without getting into too much engineering and physics, just know that three-wire configurations are used in well pumps that demand more power to start and require motors larger than 1.5 HP (horsepower).
All well pumps will have a ground wire, but when distinguishing between the two types of submersible pumps, the ground wire is not counted.

Two-Wire Pumps

With the way 2-wire submersible pumps are configured, the motor will be connected by two main (black/black, black/red, black/white, etc..) wires and a green ground wire. The starting components will be located inside the motor itself, making it much more difficult to access after installation should you ever need to inspect or repair the pump. In this case, you would have to pull the pump out of its installation location in order to inspect it.

But, because everything is built inside the pump casing, installation is easier and faster as well. Two-wire pumps are also less likely to fail because of the way they are built and have fewer components overall. However, if a problem does occur, they are much more expensive to repair or replace.

2 Wire Pumps Pros Cons
Installation Easy and fast installation; no control box needed Need to pull the pump for repairs and inspections
Repairs Fewer parts; less chance of failure because parts are sealed in pump casing More time consuming/expensive to service or repair
Maintenance Designed for less overall maintenance The motor needs to be replaced if broken; can't repair individual parts that fail
Cost/Life Span Less expensive up-front costs Shorter overall lifespan

Three Wire Pumps

In a 3-wire well pump configuration, the motor is connected by a black, red, yellow, and green (ground) wire. Three-wire pumps utilize a separate control box outside the pump that houses the important starting components, which is usually mounted on a nearby wall or strut above the ground. This makes installation more intensive, however, it makes it easier and cheaper to maintain and replace individual parts instead of the entire motor if something ever goes wrong.

Though it is true that 3-wire pump configurations have more parts and thus a higher chance of needing repair and maintenance, the separate control box makes it simple to access the main components without having to remove the well pump from its installation location. Additionally, because there are more parts in a 3-wire system, it's possible that repairs would be cheaper and faster to fix since only one part of the system may need to work done, as opposed to the entire motor needing replacement in a 2-wire pump.

3 Wire Pumps Pros Cons
Installation Easy access to starting components for maintenance/repairs Less convenient and more difficult to install
Repairs Don't need to remove the pump for inspections/repair May need to repair more often
Maintenance Can replace individual parts, instead of the whole motor More parts; a higher chance of failure
Cost/Life Span Longer lifespan A more expensive up-front cost
Power More Powerful; required for more powerful 1.5HP+ motors Uses more electricity
